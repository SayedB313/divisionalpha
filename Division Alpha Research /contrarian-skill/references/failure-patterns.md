# Common Business Failure Patterns

A reference catalog of recurring failure modes. Not every pattern applies to every idea — use judgment.

## Market Failures

- **Vitamin vs. Painkiller**: The product is nice-to-have, not must-have. Users express interest in surveys but won't pay.
- **Timing Mirage**: "The market is ready NOW" has been said about every market that wasn't ready. Cloud computing was "ready" for a decade before AWS.
- **TAM Fantasy**: The addressable market is calculated by multiplying large populations by optimistic willingness-to-pay. Real SAM is often 1-5% of claimed TAM.
- **The Wedge That Doesn't Expand**: Initial niche is too small to sustain a business, and the path to broader markets is hand-waved.
- **Category Creation Costs**: If no category exists, someone has to educate the market. That's expensive and usually the first mover pays while the fast follower profits.
- **The Price Psychology Trap**: A price gap in the market may exist for structural reasons, not because nobody thought of it. The gap between free and premium often exists because the middle doesn't produce enough commitment to work.

## Product Failures

- **Feature, Not Product**: The core offering could be a feature inside an existing platform. If Slack/Google/Apple added this in a settings menu, would anyone need a standalone product?
- **Cold Start / Chicken-and-Egg**: Network effects require critical mass. How do you get value for user #1 when you need user #100?
- **Complexity Creep**: MVP is simple, but every user request adds complexity. Within 12 months the product is a Frankenstein.
- **The "Just" Problem**: "We just need to..." is always hiding massive execution complexity.
- **Retention Decay**: Initial engagement is high because of novelty. The real test is month 3-6 when the new-product dopamine wears off.
- **The Circular Dependency**: "X prevents churn" but X requires the thing that churn destroys. E.g., "community bonds prevent churn" but bonds require people who don't churn.

## Business Model Failures

- **Unit Economics Denial**: Revenue per customer doesn't cover cost to serve after accounting for support, infrastructure scaling, and churn replacement.
- **Pricing Ceiling**: Customers won't pay what the model requires. "They pay $X for Y, so they'll pay $Z for our thing" ignores that customers don't think that way.
- **Churn Death Spiral**: Monthly churn of 10%+ means you're replacing your entire customer base annually. Growth requires exponentially increasing acquisition.
- **Hidden Costs**: The financial model omits support costs, infrastructure scaling, payment processing fees, legal/compliance, and the founder's opportunity cost.
- **Subsidy Masquerading as Business**: Prices are set to acquire customers, not to sustain a business. "We'll raise prices later" rarely works.
- **The Margin Illusion**: Gross margins look incredible because founder labor is valued at $0. Include real labor costs and the picture changes dramatically.

## Competitive Failures

- **Incumbent Blindness**: "Big companies are too slow to react." They're also too big to kill. Google's first version of anything is mediocre — their fourth version eliminates competitors.
- **The Funded Competitor You Haven't Seen**: Someone with $50M in VC funding is building the same thing right now. You just don't know about them yet.
- **Switching Cost Miscalculation**: The competitor's product is worse, but switching requires effort. Most people choose "good enough and already here" over "better but I have to move."
- **Moat Delusion**: "Our moat is X" when X can be replicated in 3-6 months by any competent team.
- **Empty Quadrant Fallacy**: "Nobody is doing X+Y combined" may mean nobody has figured out how to make X+Y work together, not that there's an opportunity.

## Execution Failures

- **Solo Founder Ceiling**: One person can build an MVP but can't do sales, support, product, engineering, marketing, and fundraising simultaneously.
- **Technical Debt Trap**: Ship fast to validate -> accumulate debt -> can't iterate because everything is held together with tape.
- **Premature Scaling**: Hiring/spending before product-market fit. Every dollar spent before PMF is a bet, not an investment.
- **Founder-Market Fit Gap**: The founder's skills don't match what this specific idea requires. An engineer trying to build a community product. A marketer trying to build a technical platform.
- **Community Business Underestimation**: Community products require 5-10x more human touch than SaaS. The founder who thinks "I'll automate this" discovers that community IS the manual work.

## Psychological Failures

- **Confirmation Bias in Research**: Research that starts with a conclusion finds evidence to support it. Counter-evidence is acknowledged briefly, then ignored.
- **Survivorship Bias in Benchmarking**: Citing successful analogues while ignoring the graveyard of failures that tried the same approach.
- **Optimism in Projections**: Conservative/moderate/optimistic scenarios where "conservative" is actually "mildly optimistic."
- **Social Proof Substituting for Demand**: "People tell me they love this idea" is not the same as "people have paid me money for this."
- **Sunk Cost Escalation**: The more research you do, the harder it is to walk away — even when the research itself suggests you should.
- **The Research Trap**: 210 pages of research can create a false sense of certainty. Volume of analysis is not the same as validation. Only the market validates.
